The Fall

Enlightenment is simply knowing the truth, not in the head but with one’s whole being, knowing that “this is it.” It’s wonderful.

One mark of maturing practice is to see that life is always totally a crisis and totally not a crisis: they’re the same thing. In a mature practice, we practice just as hard whether there’s a crisis or not. Crisis or no crisis, we just do it.

Nothing is really solved until we understand that there is no solution. We’re falling, and there’s no answer to that. We can’t control it. We’re spending our life trying to stop the falling; yet it never stops.

In a sense, our life has no duration whatsoever: we’re always living the same second. There’s nothing but that second, the timeless present moment. Without that realization, each second is misery. Most human beings spend fifty to ninety percent or more of their time in their imagination, living in fantasy. We think about what has happened to us, what might have happened, how we feel about it, how we should be different, how others should be different, how it’s all a shame, and on and on; it’s all fantasy, all imagination. Memory is imagination. Every memory that we stick to devastates our life.
